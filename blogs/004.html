Some loose thoughts about the books I read this year...


Required Reading vs Fun Reading

I've internally debated this notion of required reading vs fun reading this whole year when choosing what to read. There are certain forms of books that are super easy to process and incredibly enjoyable to quickly binge. "Project Hail Mary" is maybe the perfect example of this - a fun sci-fi thriller that can easily be binged. On the other side of the spectrum "The Vital Question" was quite in-depth and took mental will power to continue (even when the content was incredibly illuminating).

I want to propose a third category of book that lies somewhere between required reading and fun reading: I want to call these books classics but am not totally sold on the terminology. Incredibly read-able books that also manage to convey deep ideas to the reader are quite uncommon and should be celebrated. Only a couple books I read this year managed to accomplish this fully and all 3 are at the top of the list for my favorites.

Above I've listed a loose chart showing my thoughts for 3 categories: technology, mountaineering, and autobiographies. This point still isn't fully fleshed out and obviously there's a spectrum to both the quality and style of each book. "Conquistadors of the Useless" and "Becoming" are probably much closer to classics than this chart suggests and "Pieces of Action" debatably doesn't have the quality to be on this chart at all.

Also, this chart fails to highlight books that break away from categorization. Is "Ministry for the Future" actually sci-fi when it touches on so many various topics including conservationism, financial systems, activism, and history? If you remove a few glorious delusional ramblings from "Kiss or Kill" - couldn't it be considered an autobiography? Besides witty tech talk - is "Project Hail Mary" anything except well written sci-fi movie bait?

Macro Optimism

To be fully honest in my biases I tend to heavily prefer books that are optimistic rather than pessimistic. I believe that all novels (despite maybe some of the most factual) can choose to portray their purpose in whatever skew they choose.

"Age of Ambition" is a perfect example of this - Evan Osnos tackles a complex topic of modern China and tells an optimistic story while not shying away from all the problems that existed at the time. There is a serious caveat to my above statement in that the book was written in 2012 - much of the current issues with China are different than they were in 2012 and I would hope a book written today would still fully address everything wrong with modern China without losing the optimism.

On the flipside, "Where is my Flying Car?" struck me as remarkably pessimistic about progress and choose to highlight the places we've went wrong rather than advocate for what we can do better. The author constantly remarks about the negative effects of regulation without addressing the deeper issues of why the regulation was needed in the first place or how effective regulation could be created in the future.

"Gold, Oil, and Avocados" was the lone pessimistic book I read this year that I fully enjoyed and I think that may be influenced by the nature of topics covered. It's hard to tell the history of Latin America through commodities without telling a pessimistic narrative. I'm sure that in a book talking about the future rather than the past there'd be a lot more room for optimism and the author taught me a huge amount about Latin America as a whole from this book.

Individualism

Loosely defined as "a social theory favoring freedom of action for individuals over collective control." This social theory has become the dominant way of thinking in modern America and defines much of today's society. I've always been an ardent supporter of individualism in how I act and see the world, but I found it really interesting that many of my favorite books this year directly pushed back against this concept.

"Cloud Atlas" was the most direct attack against individualism arguing in a really nuanced way that an individualistic society will always move towards disorder and loss. I particularly enjoyed the method of storytelling to reach this conclusion and was not aware of the full purpose of the novel until the final chapters. The movie may have missed much of this subtlety based on reviews but I can always avoid the movie and revere the book.

"Ministry for the Future" only really reached a finale once the earth had jointly decided to approach consumption, success, economies, and progress in a collective way rather than an individualist one. I'm in agreement with much of the book and also do not see a way through the coming climate crisis without rethinking much of our current society.

On a slightly different note, "Piranesi" showed the fragility of the individual and how certain experiences can totally reshape who we are and what our narrative that we tell ourselves is. How can we hold up individualism without addressing the fragility of this approach?
